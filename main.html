The central tension: Alternative data adoption is at an all-time high, yet alpha derived from it is eroding at the fastest rate on record. Funds that once had a 6-month edge from a dataset now have weeks. The market needs synthesis and novelty, not just more raw data feeds.

  • Hedge funds are the core market. They account for 68% of all alternative data end-users and are the most acquisitive buyers — Morgan Stanley estimates serious funds spend $1M per $1B AUM in year 1, scaling to $3M annually by year 3. A $5B fund builds toward $15M/year in data costs.

  • Web-scraped data is the dominant signal type. It appears in 56% of investment cases and is used by 59% of advisers to train custom AI systems. Job postings and pricing pages are particularly high-value signals for pre-earnings prediction.

  • The barrier to entry is collapsing — fast. What cost $500,000/year in 2015 can now be accessed for $5,000 in 2026. Smaller RIAs and independent analysts are entering the market for the first time, creating a downstream buyer segment below the institutional tier.

  • Alpha decay is the defining challenge of 2026. Research shows that predictive signals lose 5–10% of their effectiveness annually in liquid markets. Institutional portfolio convergence — measured by pairwise cosine similarity of holdings — has increased 42% since 2013, directly correlated with AI adoption waves.

  • AI integration is creating a new layer of demand. 93% of investment advisers plan to grow AI budgets in 2026, yet only 31% have adopted AI-processed data to optimize strategies directly. This gap — between AI spend intent and actual deployment — represents an actionable whitespace for a synthesis platform.

  • M&A signals strategic importance. BlackRock's $3.2B acquisition of Preqin and S&P Global's acquisition of With Intelligence in October 2025 confirm that alternative data is moving from niche tool to institutional infrastructure. Consolidation is underway; the window for independent platforms is narrowing.

03

Buyer & Business Profile

Primary Buyer — Institutional

  • Who: Equity long/short and event-driven hedge funds, $500M–$10B AUM
  • Title: Fundamental analysts, sector PMs, research leads
  • Trigger: Need earnings edge 4–8 weeks before quarterly report
  • Budget authority: Research budget, $50K–$500K annually for alt data
  • Decision cycle: 2–4 months; requires compliance sign-off on data provenance
  • Spends today on: 3–8 separate vendor relationships for different signal types

Secondary Buyer — Emerging

  • Who: Smaller RIAs, independent analysts, family offices, sub-$500M funds
  • Title: Founder-PM, analyst teams of 1–3 people
  • Trigger: Want institutional-quality signals without institutional budgets
  • Budget authority: Direct; decisions made in days not months
  • Decision cycle: 1–2 weeks; price-sensitive; prefer SaaS models
  • Pain: Currently excluded from $50K+ data feeds entirely

Business Characteristics

  • Geography: 56.8% of market is North America; NYC and London are anchor markets
  • Industry: BFSI dominates with 16.5% revenue share; hedge funds alone = 68% of users
  • Spend cadence: Annual contracts; mid-year expansions common when signals prove out
  • Key metric they track: Earnings surprise rate, signal-to-noise ratio, latency to insight
  • 2025 budget growth: 89% of advisers planned to grow alt data budgets in 2026

What a Winning Pitch Looks Like

  • Lead with backtests: Show accuracy against historical earnings surprises on 20–30 companies
  • Compliance package first: MNPI clearance is a blocker; address it before the demo
  • API-first framing: "Integrates with your existing workflow" beats "new dashboard to check"
  • Signal novelty: Prove the signals are not already in YipitData or Bloomberg
  • Time-to-signal: Show how many days before earnings the signal fires reliably

04

Real Customer Pain Points

Drawn from industry reports, hedge fund practitioner commentary, and vendor positioning. These are the complaints that lead funds to switch or trial new solutions.

01

Signal crowding kills the edge within weeks

The datasets everyone buys — YipitData's transaction feed, SimilarWeb's traffic — are now priced into markets within days of availability. Predictive signals lose 5–10% of effectiveness annually. Funds report that data that generated 6-month alpha in 2020 now lasts 2–3 weeks before being arbitraged away.

→ Opportunity: novel synthesis, not another raw feed
02

Data fragmentation across 3–8 vendors with no unified view

A typical fund tracking one company might use Thinknum for jobs, SimilarWeb for traffic, a credit card panel for transactions, and a news sentiment feed — all from different vendors, with different schemas, update cadences, and formats. There is no single "company dashboard." Analysts build this manually in Excel or internal tools.

→ Opportunity: unified, per-company signal aggregation
03

Cost structures exclude the fastest-growing buyer segment

Quality satellite data runs $50K–$500K/year. Credit card panels are $100K–$1M+. Job data feeds range from $20K–$200K. Smaller funds and independent analysts — whose numbers are growing rapidly as the barrier to entry falls — are structurally priced out of the market at these tiers. Two-thirds of institutional advisers spend over $1M/year; almost no sub-$500M fund can justify that.

→ Opportunity: accessible SaaS tier at $500–$2K/month
04

Raw data requires 3–6 months of data engineering before it's usable

Funds consistently cite integration overhead as the hidden cost of alt data. A clean, well-documented API can reduce time-to-signal dramatically, but most vendors deliver flat files or inconsistent schemas. The engineering bottleneck means many funds evaluate a dataset for months before deriving any value — and many never complete the integration.

→ Risk to address: must deliver clean, documented, interpretable output
05

Legal ambiguity around MNPI creates compliance paralysis

The SEC's treatment of alternative data sits in a grey zone: public web data is generally legal; scraped data derived from a breach of duty is not. The line is blurry enough that compliance teams at larger funds regularly block or delay alt data purchases. Vendors that provide clear data provenance documentation, legal opinions, and audit trails win deals that others lose on compliance grounds alone.

→ Risk to address: provenance and MNPI clearance is a must-have, not a nice-to-have
06

Signals are noisy: too many false positives before earnings

A single spike in job postings could mean expansion or just a seasonal pattern. A discount on a pricing page could be a promotional campaign or a sign of distress. Analysts report that the hardest part of using web-scraped data is distinguishing meaningful signal from noise — requiring either deep domain expertise or cross-signal validation. Most platforms sell the raw data and leave this problem entirely to the buyer.

→ Opportunity: cross-signal scoring to surface only high-confidence changes
07

Coverage is narrow: signals exist for large caps, not mid- and small-cap

YipitData covers 1,000+ companies; most niche providers cover far fewer. The Russell 2000 — where information asymmetry and earnings surprises are most exploitable — has very limited alt data coverage. Funds running small-cap strategies face a coverage desert. The MVP has a genuine opening here if it can scale web signal collection beyond the S&P 500 coverage that dominates the market.

→ Opportunity: mid/small-cap coverage as a differentiation vector
08

No early warning system: signals fire too close to the earnings date

The most valuable signal fires 4–8 weeks before earnings. Most platforms deliver data with 1–2 week latency, or normalize it in ways that remove the early-warning edge. Funds want to know in late February what a company's March quarter will look like — not in April after the fact. Latency and signal timing are as important as accuracy.

→ Opportunity: lead-time tracking and signal-fire alerting as core features
